The science: bent light, a lopsided ring, and honest caveats

1 · Every pixel is a geodesic

The camera fires one ray of light per pixel, backwards into the scene. Around a non-rotating black hole, spacetime is Schwarzschild’s 1916 solution, and light obeys the exact null-geodesic orbit equation d²u/dφ² = −u + (3/2) rsu² (u = 1/r). Each ray’s conserved impact parameter is set exactly from the camera’s position — b = r sinψ/√(1−rs/r) — and then the equation is integrated step by step with fourth-order Runge–Kutta. A ray ends one of three ways: it crosses the glowing disk (a pixel of hot gas), it climbs back out to the stars (a pixel of lensed sky), or it spirals inside the photon sphere and never returns (a pixel of shadow). Nothing else decides the image.

2 · Why the picture looks like that

The bright halo hugging the shadow is the photon ring: rays with impact parameter near b = 3√3⁄2 rs ≈ 2.598 rs circle the hole one or more times before escaping, so a thin annulus of sky carries an image of the entire disk, repeated. The disk’s far side doesn’t hide behind the hole: rays passing over the pole are bent down onto its top surface, so you see it arched above the shadow (and its underside mirrored below — the Interstellar look). And the two wings are unequal because the gas orbits at nearly half the speed of light at the inner edge: relativistic Doppler beaming brightens the approaching side by roughly g⁴, where g is the frequency shift. Toggle the effects off one at a time and the portrait falls apart into a Newtonian saucer.

3 · The disk

The gas is a thin Shakura–Sunyaev disk: temperature T ∝ r−3/4(1−√(rin/r))1/4, truncated at the innermost stable circular orbit rin = 3 rs, radiating as a blackbody whose color you see. Each disk pixel is shifted by the exact combined factor g = √(1−3M/r) / [(1−Ω Lz/E) √(1−rs/rcam)] — orbital time dilation, Doppler from the Keplerian motion (Ω = √(M/r³)), and the climb out of the well — with observed intensity scaled by g⁴ and observed color by the blackbody at g·T. The swirling brightness pattern is decoration (real turbulence isn’t computable in a browser tab), but it is advected at the true Keplerian angular speed at every radius, so the differential shear you watch is honest.

4 · Honest caveats

No spin. This is Schwarzschild; real black holes rotate. Kerr spacetime drags frames, shrinks the ISCO (as low as 0.5 rs of gravitational radius for maximal spin), and makes the shadow slightly D-shaped — percent-level corrections to the ring, big corrections to the inner disk. The disk is opaque and thin; M87*’s real glow is synchrotron plasma, not a blackbody, and no polarization is computed here. The camera hovers — a static Schwarzschild observer, engines burning, not a free-falling one. The background sky is procedural, not a star catalog. And a confession from Hollywood: Interstellar’s Gargantua deliberately omitted Doppler beaming and blue/red shifts so the disk would look symmetric and golden on screen — the preset above reproduces that choice; flip “Doppler & beaming” back on to see what Kip Thorne’s honest version looks like.

Numerical method: per pixel, the ray’s plane is found from camera position and pixel direction (Schwarzschild orbits are planar), the Binet equation above is advanced by RK4 with dφ = 6π/steps, and disk crossings are detected by sign change of the plane-height and interpolated within the step. Escape is declared when the ray moves outward past r = max(2rcam, 400 rs); its tangent there is the sky direction (the residual bending beyond that radius is ~2Mb/r² ≲ 10⁻⁵ rad). Rays that exhaust the step budget while winding are charged to the shadow — that is what the quality slider trades.

Two engines: the GPU renders every pixel in parallel in float32 fragment shaders; the CPU engine renders the identical algorithm in float64, progressively, as the reference. The honesty panel quantifies both sins: integration error (render-grade vs 25,000-step reference ray) and arithmetic error (the same ray replayed with float32 rounding after every step).

Units: G = c = 1, lengths in Schwarzschild radii (M = ½), time in rs/c. Blackbody colors via a standard Planckian-locus fit, ACES-style tone mapping. The mass buttons change no physics — only the physical ruler: rs in kilometres, the ISCO clock, and the ring’s angular size from Earth (52 μas for Sgr A*, ~40 μas for M87* — the sizes the Event Horizon Telescope photographed).
